Freelancing in Switzerland: What the Tax Office Actually Checks

12. Februar 2026Lesezeit: 5 Min.SelbständigkeitAHVNebenberuf

More and more people in Switzerland combine a regular job with freelance work on the side. Whether you are an ML consultant, a graphic designer, or a weekend yoga instructor, the tax implications are real — and often misunderstood.

The CHF 2,300 AHV Threshold

If your total freelance income from a single client exceeds CHF 2,300 per calendar year, that client is obliged to deduct AHV contributions. But here is the part most people miss: if your total self-employment income (across all clients) exceeds CHF 2,300, you must register with SVA Zurich as self-employed and pay AHV contributions yourself.

Key rule: The CHF 2,300 threshold applies per year, not per client. If you invoice CHF 5,000 to Client A and CHF 3,000 to Client B, your total of CHF 8,000 clearly triggers the obligation — even if each client individually might not think they need to deduct.

How to Declare Freelance Income

Freelance income goes under Einkommen aus selbständiger Nebentätigkeit in the Steuererklärung. You must declare the gross amount (total of all invoices paid within the tax year) and may deduct legitimate business expenses.

What Counts as a Deductible Expense?

The tax office accepts expenses that are directly and exclusively related to earning the freelance income. Commonly accepted:

  • Equipment: Laptop, monitor, peripherals (proportional if also used privately)
  • Software subscriptions: Cloud tools, design software, development environments
  • Professional development: Courses, certifications directly related to the freelance activity
  • Home office costs: A proportional share of rent/utilities, but only if you have a dedicated workspace
  • Travel: Client meetings, conferences
Watch out: Handwritten expense lists or informal notes will generally not be accepted by the tax office as sufficient documentation. Always keep original receipts, invoices, and bank/credit card statements. The burden of proof is on you.

The Documentation Standard

The Zurich tax office has become increasingly strict about freelance expense documentation. The minimum they expect:

  • Original receipts for any expense over CHF 100
  • Bank or credit card statements corroborating the purchases
  • A clear separation between private and business use for shared items (e.g., a laptop used for both work and gaming)
  • Invoices issued to clients, showing dates, amounts, and services rendered

Cross-Validation: Invoices vs. Bank Deposits

A good practice — and something the tax office does routinely — is to cross-reference your declared freelance invoices against incoming bank deposits. If you declared CHF 22,000 in freelance income but your bank only shows CHF 18,000 in matching deposits, expect questions. The discrepancy could be due to late payments crossing the year boundary, but you should be prepared to explain it.

Example
Priya invoiced four clients for a total of CHF 22,000. Her bank statement shows three deposits totaling CHF 18,000 — the fourth client (CHF 4,000) paid in January of the following year. She should declare the CHF 22,000 (accrual basis) and note the timing difference if queried.

Mixing Employment and Self-Employment

If you have both a regular job (Lohnausweis) and freelance income, be careful not to double-count deductions. Professional expenses related to your employed position (Berufsauslagen) are separate from freelance business expenses. You cannot claim your laptop as a freelance expense if your employer already provides one for the same type of work.
