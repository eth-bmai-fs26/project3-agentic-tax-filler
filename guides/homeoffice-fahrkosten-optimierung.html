Home Office vs. Commute Deduction: Finding the Sweet Spot

3. März 2026Lesezeit: 5 Min.HomeofficeFahrkostenOptimierung

Since 2023, Zurich has allowed a Homeoffice-Pauschale of CHF 3 per day worked from home. Sounds great — but there is a catch. Every home office day reduces your commuting deduction. So when is it actually worth claiming?

The Trade-Off

The math works like this:

  • Each home office day: +CHF 3 (Homeoffice-Pauschale)
  • Each home office day: −proportional share of annual commute deduction

If your annual commute pass costs CHF 2,840 and you work 230 days per year, each commuting day is worth about CHF 12.35. Working from home saves you from commuting — but costs you that CHF 12.35 deduction while only giving back CHF 3.

The math is clear: For most employees with public transport passes, the home office deduction (CHF 3/day) is worth less than the proportional commuting deduction (~CHF 10–15/day). Claiming home office days can actually reduce your total deduction.

When Home Office IS Worth Claiming

The calculation flips in specific situations:

  • Short commutes with cheap passes: If your annual pass costs under ~CHF 700, the per-day commuting value drops below CHF 3 and home office becomes favorable
  • Car commuters at the cantonal cap: If your commuting deduction is already at the maximum cap, additional commuting days don't increase it — so home office days are pure upside
  • Very frequent home office: If you work from home 4 days/week, you may want to consider not buying an annual pass at all — then the calculation changes entirely

The Break-Even Formula

Formula
Break-even: when annual pass cost ÷ total work days = CHF 3

If annual pass = CHF 690 and work days = 230:
CHF 690 ÷ 230 = CHF 3.00 → exactly break-even

Below CHF 690: home office days are beneficial
Above CHF 690: home office days reduce your total deduction

The Agent's Role

A good tax agent should:

  1. Ask how many days per week the taxpayer works from home
  2. Calculate both scenarios (with and without home office claim)
  3. Recommend whichever produces the higher total deduction
  4. Explain the trade-off clearly so the taxpayer understands
Don't assume: Just because someone works at a tech company with flexible policies doesn't mean they work from home. Always ask — never guess.
