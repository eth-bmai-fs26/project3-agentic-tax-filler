Retirement and Taxes in Zurich: Capital vs. Pension — What Goes Where?

28. Januar 2026Lesezeit: 5 Min.PensionierungKapitalbezugBVG

Retirement is one of the most complex tax events in a Swiss taxpayer's life. The year you retire, you may have salary income, AHV pension, BVG pension, and a lump-sum capital withdrawal — all taxed differently. Getting the form placement wrong is a costly mistake.

The Four Income Streams in a Retirement Year

Income TypeForm SectionTax Treatment
Employment salary (pre-retirement months)Unselbständige ErwerbstätigkeitOrdinary income tax rate
AHV pensionRenten / PensionenOrdinary income tax rate
BVG pension (monthly)Renten / PensionenOrdinary income tax rate
Capital withdrawal from PensionskasseKapitalbezüge (separate form)Separate, reduced rate

The Most Common Mistake

The single most frequent error we see: declaring the Pensionskasse capital withdrawal as ordinary income. This is wrong and extremely costly. A CHF 250,000 capital withdrawal taxed at ordinary rates could cost tens of thousands more than it should.

Critical rule: Capital withdrawals from the Pensionskasse are taxed separately from all other income, at a significantly reduced rate. In ZHprivateTax, this goes on the dedicated Kapitalbezüge form — NOT in the regular income section.

What About the Freizügigkeitskonto?

If you have a vested benefits account (Freizügigkeitskonto), the rules are straightforward but often confused:

  • The balance as of December 31st must be declared as wealth
  • Any interest earned during the year is income (Vermögensertrag)
  • The capital itself is NOT income — it is just an asset sitting in your wealth declaration
  • When you eventually withdraw it, that withdrawal will be taxed as a Kapitalbezug (separate, reduced rate)
Don't forget: If you retired mid-year, you can still contribute to Pillar 3a for that year (up until age 65 for men, 64 for women). Many retirees miss this final contribution window — it's the last chance to claim the deduction.

Worked Example: Thomas, Age 64

Example
Thomas retired in September after 35 years at Zurich Insurance. His tax situation:

Ordinary income:
• 8 months salary: CHF 96,000
• 4 months AHV pension: CHF 9,560
• 4 months BVG pension: CHF 12,800
Total ordinary: CHF 118,360

Separate taxation:
• Capital withdrawal: CHF 250,000 → goes on Kapitalbezüge form

If Thomas had mistakenly added the CHF 250,000 to ordinary income, his marginal tax rate would have jumped dramatically. The separate taxation saves him roughly CHF 25,000–35,000 in taxes.
